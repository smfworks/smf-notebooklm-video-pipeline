This is a WisdomForge lesson on Seneca's teaching: "It is not that we have a short time to live, but that we waste a great deal of it. Life is long enough if it is well spent."

Structure:
1. Open with the quote and briefly identify Seneca — a philosopher, dramatist, and advisor to Emperor Nero who wrote about time as our most precious and most wasted resource
2. Explain the core principle: Seneca argues we don't have too little time — we squander it. We treat time as infinite while guarding money, property, and status as though they were scarce. The shortness of life is a perception problem, not a time problem
3. Show 2-3 specific modern applications: the person who says "I'll start next Monday" and loses years to procrastination, the professional who fills every hour with busyness but never does what actually matters to them, the habit of doom-scrolling instead of doing one meaningful thing with the next thirty minutes
4. Close with a practical challenge: tonight, write down the one thing you've been saying you "don't have time for" — then schedule thirty minutes for it tomorrow

Tone: Warm and direct. Speak to a curious adult learner, not an academic. Use concrete examples, not abstractions. Avoid jargon. The goal is insight, not information.

Focus: Make the ancient principle feel immediately useful. The viewer should finish thinking "I can try this today."
