# The Dichotomy of Control: What Is in Our Power

**Knowledge Category:** Core Concepts
**Age Adaptability:** Yes
**Audio Version:** Not yet
**Last Updated:** 2026-04-24

## Summary

The Dichotomy of Control is Epictetus's most famous and influential teaching: some things are within our power, and some things are not. Learning this distinction—and actually living by it—is, in Epictetus's view, the foundation of freedom, happiness, and the good life. This section unpacks what belongs inside and outside our sphere of control, why this matters for how we spend our mental energy, and how to apply it when life gets messy.

## Content

### Where It All Begins: The Opening Words of the *Enchiridion*

Imagine walking into a classroom where the teacher clears their throat, looks you dead in the eye, and opens with this:

> Some things are within our power, while others are not. Within our power are opinion, impulse, desire, and aversion—in short, whatever is our own doing. Not within our power are our body, our property, reputation, offices—in short, whatever is not our own doing. (*Enchiridion* 1, trans. Hard)

That is not an introductory throat-clearing. That is the whole game. Epictetus—or more precisely, his student Arrian who recorded his teachings—puts this right at the front door. Everything else in Stoicism flows from here. If you only ever remember one thing about Epictetus, remember this: there is a line between what you can control and what you cannot, and your peace of mind depends on which side of that line you invest your emotions in.

So what counts as "within our power"? Epictetus lists: opinion (*hypolepsis*), impulse (*horme*), desire (*orexis*), and aversion (*ekklisis*). These are not random items. They are all functions of your reasoning faculty—your capacity to judge, want, reject, and move toward something. In other words: your *mind's* activities. You can choose what to believe about a situation. You can choose what to want and what to avoid. You can choose whether to act or not. These are yours.

What is *not* within your power? Your body. Your property. Your reputation. Political offices. Whether other people like you. Whether it rains on your wedding day. Whether you get the job. Whether your child gets sick. In short: everything that depends on factors outside your own reasoning and choice.

This is not a suggestion. It is a structural fact about the world. And Epictetus thinks most human suffering comes from pretending the structural fact is not true.

### Why the Body Is Not Ours

This one trips people up immediately. "Wait," you might say, "my arm is literally attached to me. How is that not mine?"

Epictetus has an answer, and it is chillingly practical. In *Discourses* 1.1, he asks us to imagine wanting something that is not in our power to obtain. Let's say you want your body to be perfectly healthy forever. Can you guarantee that? No. Disease, accident, and age can strike without your consent. You can exercise, eat well, sleep enough—and still get sick. You do not have final say over your body. It is "not your own doing."

Or take property. You can buy a house. You can lock the doors. But fire, flood, war, or a market crash can take it from you. Even your own death will separate you from it. So is it truly in your power? Not in the sense Epictetus means. It is subject to forces beyond your reasoned choice.

The same goes for reputation. You can act honorably. You cannot force others to interpret your actions honorably. You cannot stop someone from gossiping about you or misrepresenting your motives. Your reputation lives in other people's minds—which is to say, in places you do not own and cannot govern.

Epictetus drives the point home in *Discourses* 2.5:

> It is not the things themselves that disturb people, but their judgments about the things.

The implication is radical. The world does not break you. Your *judgment* about the world does. And because your judgment is within your power, you are never truly powerless.

### The Internal: Opinion, Impulse, Desire, Aversion

Let's slow down and look at what Epictetus says *is* in our power. These four terms deserve attention because modern readers often flatten them into "my thoughts and feelings." It is closer to the truth, but not quite.

**Opinion** (*hypolepsis*): This is your assent to a proposition. When something happens—say, someone cuts you off in traffic—you immediately form an impression: "That driver is a jerk." That is an automatic impression. But your *opinion* is whether you fully buy into it. Do you assent to the thought "This person is evil and out to ruin my morning"? Or do you pause and say, "Actually, I don't know why they did that. Maybe they're rushing to the hospital. Maybe they just made a mistake." The impression arrives unbidden. The assent is yours.

**Impulse** (*horme*): This is the tendency to act toward something. When you feel hungry, you get an impulse to eat. When you see someone suffering, you get an impulse to help. These impulses do not have to be obeyed blindly. You can examine an impulse before acting on it. Is this impulse reasonable? Does it align with virtue? Does it fit the situation? That examination is within your power.

**Desire** (*orexis*): This is wanting something to happen. It is future-oriented. You desire good health, success, love, security. Epictetus's instruction is simple and brutal: do not desire what is not in your power. Desiring things outside your control sets you up for disappointment, fear, and manipulation. Instead, desire what is genuinely up to you: to act with integrity, to judge wisely, to meet each moment with your best self.

**Aversion** (*ekklisis*): This is the flip side of desire—wanting something *not* to happen. Do not fear what is not in your power. Fear of death, poverty, pain, or disapproval is fear of externals. You cannot guarantee you will avoid these things, so fearing them invests your emotional energy in outcomes you cannot secure. Instead, avert yourself from vice. Turn away from dishonesty, from cruelty, from rashness. Those are within your power to avoid.

### Why Externals Are Called "Indifferent"

This is another hard teaching. If I lose my house, my job, or a loved one, most people would say that is genuinely bad. Epictetus says: these things are "indifferent" (*adiaphora*).

He does not mean you do not care about them. He means they are indifferent *to virtue*. They do not make you a better or worse person by themselves. What matters is what you do with your own mind in relation to them. Do you respond to loss with rage and despair? Or with courage, acceptance, and continued integrity?

Think of it this way: health, wealth, and good reputation are like game pieces. They are convenient to have, but they are not *you*. The *player* is your character—your capacity for wisdom, justice, courage, and self-discipline. The player is what matters. The pieces come and go.

In *Discourses* 1.29, Epictetus uses vivid metaphors. He compares life to a banquet. When a dish is passed to you, you take a reasonable portion. If it never reaches you, you do not grab for it or grieve. You wait politely. If it is taken away before you are done, you let it go. You do not own the dish. You were a guest at the table.

> Remember that you must behave in life as you would at a banquet. Is anything brought round to you? Put out your hand and take a moderate share. Does it pass you by? Do not stop it. Is it not yet come? Do not yearn in desire toward it, but wait till it reaches you. (Discourses 2.5, trans. Matheson)

This is not passive resignation. It is strategic clarity about where your control begins and ends.

### Prohairesis: The Faculty of Choice

Now for a scholarly nuance. Where does all this control live? Epictetus uses a specific word: *prohairesis* (προαίρεσις). This is often translated as "moral character," "volition," or "faculty of choice." It is the part of you that judges, assents, rejects, and decides. It is uniquely yours and cannot be taken away by anyone—not a tyrant, not a torturer, not circumstance.

A. A. Long, in *Epictetus: A Stoic and Socratic Guide to Life*, points out that *prohairesis* is central to Epictetus's entire ethical system. It is the "I" that remains free regardless of chains, loss, or death. The Roman Emperor Marcus Aurelius, who read Epictetus, echoed this when he wrote that the mind can be undisturbed even when the body is in pain.

Keith Seddon, in *Epictetus' Handbook and the Tablet of Cebes*, emphasizes that *prohairesis* is not a mystical soul. It is your reasoning faculty in action. When Epictetus says externals are not in our power, he is not saying you should be a robot. He is saying: anchor your identity and your peace in the part of you that reason governs. Everything else is a welcome guest but not the host.

### Practical Application: A Day in the Life

How do you actually *do* this? Let's walk through a day.

**Morning.** You wake up. You check your phone. There is an email: your project was rejected. Immediate impression: "I am a failure." Before you assent to that, you pause. Is it true that a rejected project makes you a failure? No. It means the project did not land this time. Your worth is in your effort, your learning, your continued willingness to show up. That is in your power. The market's response is not.

**Commute.** Traffic is terrible. Somebody cuts you off. Impression: "What an idiot. I am so angry." Pause. Is anger within your power? Yes—meaning, you can choose not to assent to the thought that this is a catastrophe worth rage. The driver's behavior is not in your power. Your response is. You take a breath.

**Work.** A colleague takes credit for your idea in a meeting. Your reputation feels threatened. Impression: "I need everyone to know that was mine." Pause. You cannot control what people believe. You can control whether you speak with clarity and integrity. You can choose to address it calmly later, or let it go, secure in the knowledge that your effort is what counts.

**Evening.** News of an illness in the family. Terrifying. Painful. Epictetus is not saying "don't care." He is saying: care, and then direct your care toward what you can do. Can you be present? Can you offer comfort? Can you accept the uncertainty without collapsing? Those responses are in your power. The outcome of the illness is not.

The key move is this: every time you feel distress, ask, "What am I trying to control that is not mine?" And every time you feel helpless, ask, "What is still in my power right now?"

### Common Objections and Misunderstandings

**"This sounds like toxic positivity."**

No. Epictetus does not say "just think positive." He says: examine your judgments. If something truly bad happens, it is appropriate to feel grief, sadness, or concern. The question is whether you add *another* layer of suffering by insisting the universe should not have allowed it. You can grieve a loss without believing the loss is an injustice against you personally.

**"This means I shouldn't try to change anything."**

Wrong. You should act. You should vote, work, love, build, protect, and strive. But you should act with *resignation of the outcome*—not passive resignation of effort, but active acceptance that results are not yours to command. You do your best, and you release attachment to the result. As Epictetus says in *Discourses* 2.5: demand not that things happen as you wish, but wish them to happen as they do, and you will go on well.

**"So feelings don't matter?"**

Feelings matter enormously. But feelings arise from judgments. If you want to change how you feel, change your judgments. That work is within your power. Denying feelings is not Stoic. Understanding where they come from and whether they are reasonable—that is Stoic.

**"What about social justice? If I accept everything, won't I tolerate injustice?"**

Epictetus was a slave. He knew injustice intimately. Acceptance of externals does not mean acceptance of *wrongdoing*. It means you battle injustice from a place of inner clarity rather than from rage that clouds your judgment. You still fight. But you fight because it is right, not because you are guaranteed victory.

### The Freedom Nobody Can Take

Here is the breathtaking promise at the heart of the dichotomy: if you only desire what is in your power, you are always successful. If you only fear what is in your power, you are fearless. Your boss cannot make you miserable unless you *choose* to believe your happiness depends on their approval. Death cannot terrorize you if you have already accepted mortality as outside your control.

This is why Epictetus can say, in *Discourses* 1.1:

> No one is free who does not command himself.

And in *Discourses* 3.24:

> You are a little soul carrying around a corpse.

The body is baggage. The soul—your reasoning, choosing self—is the traveler. Travel light.

---

### A Living Philosophy, Not a Dead System

The Dichotomy of Control is not an abstract puzzle. It is a daily practice. Every annoyance, every grief, every frustration is an opportunity to ask: "What is mine here, and what is not?" Each time you answer honestly, you recover a piece of your freedom.

The modern cognitive-behavioral therapist Albert Ellis credited Epictetus with inspiring Rational Emotive Behavior Therapy (REBT). The core insight is the same: events do not disturb us; our beliefs about events disturb us. Change the belief, open the door to peace.

But Epictetus goes deeper than therapy. He is not just trying to reduce anxiety. He is offering a vision of the good life: a life where your inner citadel remains your own, no matter what storms rage outside.

## For the Guide (Socrates)

### How to Teach This

This is the first and most important concept. Spend real time here. Use the learner's own frustrations as raw material. Ask them to name something that upset them recently, then gently walk through: what part was in their power? What part was not? Do not rush to "solution." Let the distinction land emotionally, not just intellectually.

### By Age Group

**Age 5–10:**

Imagine you are playing a video game. You control your character—the jumps, the moves, the choices. But you don't control the game's weather, the surprise monsters, or whether you win. The Dichotomy of Control is like that: you control your choices and your attitude. You don't control what other people do or what happens around you. If you get mad because it rains, you're trying to control the sky. That doesn't work! But you can choose to bring an umbrella.

- Activity: Draw a circle. Inside, write things you can control (your words, your effort, your kindness). Outside, write things you can't (weather, what others think, the rules of a game). Discuss one that surprised them.

**Age 11–14:**

Think about the last time you were really frustrated. Was it because of something someone else did? Something you didn't get? Here's the thing: a lot of what upsets us is stuff we can't actually control. Epictetus says we're like people trying to steer a boat that's on land—it doesn't matter how hard you try. Instead, focus on what you *can* steer: your judgments, your effort, your reactions.

For example, if you study hard and still get a bad grade, the grade isn't fully in your control (the test questions, your mood that day, the curve). But studying hard *is*. Your response to the grade *is*. That's where your power lives.

- Questions: What is something you really want that you can't guarantee? How would it feel to still try your best without needing it to turn out a certain way?

**Age 15–18+:**

Epictetus opens the *Enchiridion* with what scholars consider the foundational axiom of his ethics: the distinction between what is 'up to us' (*eph' hēmin*) and what is not. The internal category—opinion, impulse, desire, aversion—maps onto what he calls *prohairesis*, the faculty of rational choice. The external category maps onto *indifferents* (*adiaphora*), things that may be preferred or dispreferred but carry no moral weight.

A scholarly debate exists: is this a strict dichotomy, or can externals influence our internal state? Epictetus seems to suggest that impressions (*phantasiai*) arise automatically, but *assent* (*sunkatathesis*) remains ours. This is the Stoic compatibilist answer to the problem of emotional life: we are not Stoic statues, but beings who interpret. The control lies in interpretation.

- Discussion prompts: Read *Enchiridion* 1 and *Discourses* 1.1 side by side. How does the fuller classroom context in the Discourses deepen the stripped-down Handbook version? Is *prohairesis* better translated as "will," "character," or "moral purpose"?

**Adult:**

This teaching is deceptively simple and brutally difficult. The adult learner likely has decades of invested belief in the idea that success, security, and love are prerequisites for peace. Epictetus asks you to invert that: peace is the prerequisite for everything else. You do not earn it through controlling outcomes. You claim it by redirecting your investment.

Practical application: Each morning, identify one external you are emotionally over-invested in (a project's success, someone's opinion, a financial outcome). Commit to acting well regarding it while releasing attachment to the result. In the evening, review: where did you try to control the uncontrollable? Where did you find freedom?

- Advanced reading: Compare Epictetus's dichotomy with Buddhist non-attachment and Viktor Frankl's logotherapy. What distinguishes Stoic *prohairesis* from existentialist "attitude"?
